FIRE is my first microcontroller-based robot. It took around eight months to build — which is what happens when your planning process is “vibes first, logic later.” It started as an ambitious eight-motor walker, slowly devolved into a four-motor walker, then a two-motor walker, and finally surrendered and became a roller.

The main culprit? My brilliant decision to buy four wiper motors (Rs. 300 each) without understanding anything about their mechanical quirks. Their off-center shafts turned my design into a permanent balancing act. Half my “best ideas” required metal machining equipment I absolutely did not own — unless you count a hacksaw and inflated confidence.

At one point, I even removed the internal gears from two motors and slapped bike chain sprockets onto them. It looked incredible — like a baby battle tank — and performed like absolute garbage. The wheels spun so fast they had zero torque, barely able to carry 200 grams beyond their own 900-gram motor weight. Basically, a gym bro skipping leg day.

Eventually, I admitted defeat: a walker just wasn’t happening. I pivoted to a rolling design, only to find more gearing issues waiting patiently to ruin my day.

The electronics journey was no kinder. I originally planned to use a neural-network processor, but reality tapped me on the shoulder and pointed me toward a microcontroller instead. For sensing, I used touch sensors for obstacles and infrared for edge detection — simple, sensible, and blessedly drama-free.

But honestly? Every failure was a lesson. I learned more about mechanics, robotics, circuits, microcontrollers, and even neural-network logic than any textbook could’ve taught me. I also discovered that India absolutely has electronics components — hidden in mysterious back-alley shops where the shopkeeper has no clue what he’s selling but will confidently swear it’s “very good, sir.”

Features

  • 8051 Microcontroller system
  • High torque motors
  • L298 Motor drivers for high current
  • Simple design
  • High load carrying capacity

Mechanics

  • Aluminium body for light weight
  • Cast iron for stronger joints
  • 12V DC Shunt Motor drawing 4A under load
  • 5V DC Gear motor for camera movement
  • Custom nylon wheels

Electronics

Microcontroller- ATMEL AT89S8252:

  • 8051 compatible
  • USART Serial Interface
  • 8k Flash Memory
  • 2k EEPROM
  • 256 bytes RAM
  • Four 8-bit bi-directional ports
  • Five interrupts

Microcontroller- ATMEL AT89S8252:

  • 8051 compatible
  • USART Serial Interface
  • 8k Flash Memory
  • 2k EEPROM
  • 256 bytes RAM
  • Four 8-bit bi-directional ports
  • Five interrupts

Motor Driver- L298 Dual Full Bridge Driver:

  • Up to 46V supply
  • Up to 6A total DC current
  • Low saturation voltage
  • Over-temperature protection
  • High noise immunity
  • Parallel output for higher current

74HC245 Octal Transceiver

  • 140mA per terminal (four pins in parallel)
  • 2–6V supply
  • Low quiescent current

Power Supply - L7805CV 5V regulator (1.5A):

  • Thermal overload protection
  • Short-circuit protection
  • 2 × 6V, 4.5A lead-acid rechargeable batteries

Sensors:

  • Touch sensors
  • Infrared edge detection
