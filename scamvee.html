Introduction

Scamvee was built as a small inspection platform designed to run along railway tracks and detect cracks or abnormal gaps. Mechanically, it shares a lot with FIRE, and the core electronics are largely identical. For circuit-level details, FIRE’s documentation is the best reference. The differences in Scamvee come mainly from the specific requirements of its track-inspection role.

Mechanics

Scamvee became an opportunity to refine aspects of FIRE’s mechanical design. FIRE’s biggest limitation was the motor configuration—its symmetric layout required awkward cutting and welding that never felt ideal. For Scamvee, car power-window motors were used instead, taking advantage of their mirrored housings to keep the mounting simple and clean.

The wheels came from a children’s tricycle, chosen for their metal hubs and rubber tires. Removing the rubber grip made them behave more like train wheels and allowed Scamvee to run reliably on a custom test track. With the rubber grip intact, it could operate on flat surfaces as well, but with four fixed wheels and no steering, maneuverability was limited.

Algorithm

The detection approach was straightforward: an IR sensor pointed toward the track monitored for surface continuity. When the sensor encountered a gap, a timer started; once the track was detected again, the timer stopped. Since Scamvee’s speed was known, the duration of the gap corresponded directly to its physical length. If the measured gap exceeded a defined threshold, the system flagged it and responded—during testing, Scamvee simply reversed back onto the track for review.
