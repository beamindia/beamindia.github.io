  • Making PCB

Printed circuit boards (PCBs) are a convenient way to build robust, compact circuits for small robots. A PCB can even double as part of the robot’s structure. Fabricating a simple board at home takes some time and care, but it allows the layout and size to be tailored directly to the design. For quick experiments, a general-purpose prototyping board is often enough; for cleaner, repeatable builds, a custom PCB is worth the effort.

Tools and Materials

  1. A phenolic laminate board (copper-clad)
  2. Ferric chloride (powder, for etching)
  3. Enamel paint, alcohol-based marker, or nail polish (for masking)
  4. A set of fine brushes (sizes 0–3), if using paint
  5. Petrol, spirit, or acetone (nail polish remover) for cleaning
  6. Hacksaw, file, and a drill with 1–5 mm bits
  7. Varnish for final protection
  8. Steel wool for surface preparation

Procedure

  1. Prepare the board - Clean the copper surface thoroughly using petrol, spirit, or acetone to remove grease and oxidation. A brief polish with steel wool helps achieve a uniform, bright surface.
  2. Mark and cut to size - Mark the required board outline and any mounting hole locations. Cut the board to shape with a hacksaw and smooth the edges with a file.
  3. Lay out the circuit - Draw or transfer the PCB layout onto the copper side. This can be done freehand for simple circuits or using a printed reference placed beside the board.
  4. Apply the resist - Use enamel paint, nail polish, or a permanent marker to cover all copper traces that must remain. A second coat improves coverage. Allow the board to dry completely before etching.
  5. Drill critical holes (optional at this stage) - For some workflows, it helps to drill a few key component or mounting holes before etching, particularly if they act as alignment references.
  6. Prepare the etchant - Fill a non-metallic tray with hot water (around 80–85 °C). Dissolve approximately 30–50 g of ferric chloride powder, stirring until fully mixed.
  7. Etch the board - Place the board copper-side up in the solution. Agitate gently so fresh solution flows over the copper surface. Continue until all unwanted copper has been removed, leaving only the masked traces. This typically takes 30–60 minutes, depending on concentration and temperature.
  8. Rinse and remove resist - Rinse the board thoroughly with water to stop the reaction. Remove the paint or marker using acetone or spirit to reveal the clean copper traces.
  9. Drill component holes - Drill the remaining component and mounting holes using suitable bit sizes (typically 1–1.5 mm for component leads). Clean up any rough edges around the holes.
  10. Solder the components - Populate the board with components and solder them in place, checking orientation and spacing as you go.
  11. Protect the copper - Once soldering is complete and the board has been cleaned of flux, apply a thin coat of varnish to any exposed copper areas to help reduce oxidation over time.

Safety and Handling

  1. Etchant safety - Ferric chloride is corrosive and can stain or damage many surfaces. Store it in a clearly labeled, airtight container and avoid skin and eye contact.
  2. Ventilation - Perform etching in a well-ventilated area and avoid inhaling fumes. Dispose of used etchant responsibly, following local guidelines for chemical waste.
  3. Tool safety - Use appropriate care when cutting, filing, and drilling the board. Secure the workpiece and wear eye protection when drilling.
